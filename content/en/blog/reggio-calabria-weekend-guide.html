Reggio Calabria is not a city that screams for attention. It lacks the glamour of Venice or the grandeur of Rome. But when you stand on the Lungomare at dusk and watch the sun sink behind the silhouette of Mount Etna, while the lights of Messina flicker to life across the strait – you understand why locals call this the most beautiful kilometre in Italy. I spent a weekend here and can confirm: two days is the absolute minimum to feel the rhythm of this city.

Day 1 – Morning: Museo Nazionale della Magna Grecia

We start with the single most important attraction in Reggio Calabria – the National Museum of Magna Graecia (MArRC), where Level D houses the Riace Bronzes. These two Greek statues from the 5th century BC, pulled from the sea off the Calabrian coast in 1972, are among the finest surviving bronzes of antiquity. They stand in a climate-controlled room that admits a maximum of 20 visitors at a time – you first pass through an air-filtering chamber (about 3 minutes), then have up to 20 minutes with the statues.

The museum is open Tuesday to Sunday, 9:00 AM to 8:00 PM (last entry at 7:30 PM). Tickets cost 10 euros, reduced (ages 18–25) 2 euros. My advice: arrive right at opening to beat the crowds. The full visit takes a comfortable 2–3 hours – beyond the Bronzes, there is a fantastic collection of Greek and Roman artefacts, the pinakes from Locri, and even an underwater archaeology section.

Tip: The ground floor cafe has views across the strait. Perfect for an espresso after the visit.

Day 1 – Afternoon: Exploring the Centro Storico

After the museum, lose yourself in the city centre. Corso Garibaldi – Reggio's main pedestrian boulevard – stretches nearly two kilometres and is ideal for an afternoon stroll. Along the way you pass the Duomo (an early 20th-century cathedral rebuilt after the devastating 1908 earthquake), the 15th-century Aragonese Castle, and dozens of small shops where local artisans sell ceramics and bergamot products – the citrus fruit that grows almost exclusively in the province of Reggio.

For lunch, look for trattorias advertising cucina tipica. Order maccheroni con sugo di capra (pasta with goat ragù) or melanzane alla parmigiana – the local aubergine version here is among the best I have tried anywhere in Italy. Portions are generous and prices are a world away from Rome – a full meal with wine runs 15–25 euros.

Day 1 – Evening: Lungomare Falcomatà and Dinner

The evening begins on the Lungomare Falcomatà – the seafront promenade that Gabriele D'Annunzio reportedly called il più bel chilometro d'Italia (the most beautiful kilometre in Italy). I walked here at sunset and understood the name immediately. Art Nouveau lampposts and palm trees line one side; on the other, the Strait of Messina stretches toward Sicily. On a clear day, Mount Etna appears so close you feel you could touch it. The sky shifts through shades of orange, pink and violet, and as darkness falls, the lights of Messina glitter across the water.

For dinner, duck into the side streets between the Lungomare and Corso Vittorio Emanuele. The local restaurants specialise in fish and seafood – order pesce spada alla reggina (swordfish Reggio-style) with capers, olives and cherry tomatoes. Pair it with a glass of local Cirò Rosato and the evening is complete.

Day 2 – Option A: Day Trip to Scilla

For your second day, I suggest one of two options. The first – and my favourite – is a trip to Scilla, a small town just 20–30 minutes by train from Reggio (tickets 3–5 euros). Scilla's train station sits barely 100 metres from the Marina Grande beach.

Scilla is three worlds in one: the Ruffo Castle perched atop a rocky headland, Marina Grande with its bright sand and turquoise water, and Chianalea – a fishing quarter listed among Italy's Borghi più belli d'Italia (most beautiful villages). Chianalea looks like a miniature Venice – pastel houses stand literally in the water, and narrow lanes end at fishing boats.

The essential stop: a panino con pesce spada – a grilled swordfish sandwich, one of the great street foods of Calabria. Swordfish season runs from May to September, when the fish migrate through the Strait of Messina. Traditional boats with tall watchtowers (passarelle) still head out for the catch, and in the evenings the fishermen return to Scilla's small harbour – a scene worth witnessing.

Lunch in Chianalea is an experience in itself. Restaurants are built on wooden platforms over the water, like stilts. You sit above the sea eating spaghetti con pesce spada e melanzane while gulls circle overhead. It does not get more Calabrian than this.

Day 2 – Option B: Arena dello Stretto and the Beach

If you prefer to stay in Reggio, head to the Arena dello Stretto in the morning – a restored amphitheatre by the sea, overlooking the strait and Sicily. In summer it hosts concerts and performances, but even off-season the atmosphere is striking. Imagine an ancient theatre with a sea view – because that is exactly what it is.

Afterwards, walk down to one of the beaches along the Lungomare. They are not paradise coves (for that, head to Tropea), but the sand is pleasant, the water is clean, and the lack of crowds lets you relax. Locals come here after work for a swim – a nice contrast to the packed beaches of Campania.

Spend the afternoon at the Museo del Bergamotto – a small museum dedicated to the bergamot citrus, which grows almost nowhere else on Earth but this strip of Calabria. You will learn why this fruit is essential to perfumery (Earl Grey tea is bergamot!) and sample local products.

Practical Tips for a Weekend in Reggio

Getting there: Reggio has its own airport (Aeroporto dello Stretto, REG) with flights to Rome and Milan. Alternatively, fly into Lamezia Terme and take the train (2.5–3 hours). From Naples, the train takes about 4.5 hours.

Where to stay: Central Reggio is compact – look for accommodation between Reggio Calabria Centrale station and the Lungomare. B&Bs and apartments cost 50–80 euros per night, 3-star hotels 80–120 euros.

When to go: The best months are May–June and September–October. In summer, temperatures hit 35–38°C and the city empties as locals flee to the beach. Winter is mild (12–15°C) and museums and restaurants operate as normal.

Budget: Reggio is one of the cheapest major cities in Italy. Espresso – 1 euro, trattoria lunch – 12–18 euros, dinner with wine – 25–40 euros, museum entry – 10 euros. A full weekend (accommodation plus meals) fits comfortably within 200–300 euros per person.

Reggio Calabria is not a postcard city. But it has something that more polished destinations lack – an authenticity you feel in every bite, every sunset, and every conversation with a local trattoria owner who insists you try just one more dish. Give this city a weekend. You will not regret it.

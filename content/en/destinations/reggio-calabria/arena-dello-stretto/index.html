The Arena dello Stretto is a modern open-air amphitheater perched on the very edge of the Strait of Messina in Reggio Calabria. Modelled on the ancient Greek theaters of Magna Graecia, it merges classical tradition with the role of a contemporary cultural venue. On a summer evening, when Sicily glitters with a thousand lights across the water and music fills the stage – it is hard to imagine a more dramatic setting anywhere in Italy.

The official name of the venue is Anfiteatro Ciccio Franco – since 2006 it has honoured local senator and activist Francesco "Ciccio" Franco. For the people of Reggio, however, it remains simply "the Arena," the beating heart of the city's summer cultural life.

History

Before the amphitheater existed, this site was occupied by the Molo di Porto Salvo – the main pier and harbour of Reggio Calabria. The earthquake of 28 December 1908, one of the most powerful in European history, completely destroyed both the pier and much of the city. The disaster claimed an estimated 100,000 lives on both sides of the Strait of Messina.

Reconstruction took decades. In place of the ruined port, the authorities decided to create a monument of an entirely different character. Architect Camillo Autore designed a marble obelisk crowned with a bronze statue of Athena Promachos. The inauguration took place in May 1932. The marble monument (cippo marmoreo) commemorated King Victor Emmanuel III, who had landed at Porto Salvo on 31 July 1900 as his first stop on Italian soil after ascending to the throne.

The bronze Athena Promachos – the warrior goddess defending the city – was sculpted by Messinese artist Antonio Bonfiglio. Originally facing the sea, in 2001 Mayor Italo Falcomatà had the statue rotated to face the city, symbolically embracing it under her protection. This small reorientation became an emblem of a new civic philosophy – the so-called Primavera di Reggio (Spring of Reggio), an era of cultural renaissance.

The amphitheater's current form, with its semicircular stepped seating cascading toward the sea, was developed in the second half of the 20th century, drawing on the theatrical traditions of Magna Graecia. The venue has been modernized and serves today as Reggio Calabria's premier outdoor performance space.

What to See

The arena takes the form of semicircular tiers descending toward the sea, capable of holding several thousand spectators. Its orientation is unique – the audience faces directly onto the Strait of Messina, with Sicily and Mount Etna as a natural backdrop. At sunset the panorama takes on golden-pink hues, and under favourable atmospheric conditions you may witness the Fata Morgana – an optical mirage that makes the Sicilian coastline appear to float above the water.

At the center of the amphitheater stands the marble monument with the statue of Athena Promachos. The goddess, depicted with helmet and shield, is one of the most recognisable symbols of Reggio Calabria. Bonfiglio rendered the figure in an authentic classical style, lending it the dynamism typical of 5th-century BC Greek art.

The Arena is an integral part of the Lungomare Falcomatà – the celebrated seafront boulevard hailed as "the most beautiful kilometer in Italy." Walking along the promenade, you can move seamlessly from the amphitheater to the Art Nouveau palazzi, the contemporary installations by Edoardo Tresoldi, and the sculptures of Rabarama.

In summer the arena comes alive with concerts, theatrical performances, film screenings (including the Reggio Calabria Filmfest), and fashion festivals such as Tesori del Mediterraneo. Since 2011 the arena has been the official home of the POP MUSIC FESTIVAL. It has also hosted RTL 102.5 summer broadcasts and the Miss Italia nel Mondo pageant.

Practical Information

Location: Lungomare Falcomatà, Reggio Calabria – the amphitheater sits on the seafront promenade, close to the Museo Nazionale della Magna Grecia
Admission: The arena is publicly accessible and free to visit; event tickets are sold by individual organizers
Best time: Summer evenings (June–September) – both for the cultural programme and the spectacular sunsets
Getting there: On foot from central Reggio (about 10 min from Reggio Calabria Centrale station) or via Reggio Lido railway station, which is right next to the Lungomare

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Arena dello Stretto open all year?

Yes, the amphitheater itself is a public space accessible year-round. However, cultural events – concerts, festivals, film screenings – take place mainly during the summer months, from June to September.

How many people does the Arena dello Stretto hold?

The amphitheater can accommodate several thousand spectators on its semicircular tiered seating. The exact capacity varies depending on the event configuration.

Why is the arena named after Ciccio Franco?

In 2006 the amphitheater was renamed Anfiteatro Ciccio Franco in memory of Senator Francesco "Ciccio" Franco, a local politician and civic activist who championed the development of Reggio Calabria.

How can I combine a visit to the Arena with other Reggio attractions?

The Arena sits directly on the Lungomare Falcomatà, just minutes on foot from the Museo Nazionale della Magna Grecia. An ideal itinerary: museum in the morning, a stroll along the boulevard, then an evening event at the arena with sunset views over the Strait of Messina.

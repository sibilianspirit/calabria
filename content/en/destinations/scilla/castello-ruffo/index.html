Castello Ruffo di Scilla is a mighty fortress built on a rocky promontory above the Strait of Messina – precisely where Homer's mythical Scylla once lurked. With over 2,500 years of history, the castle offers some of the finest panoramas in all of Calabria, spanning the Sicilian coastline, the Aeolian Islands, and the endless blue of the Tyrrhenian Sea. It is an essential stop on any visit to Scilla – both for the views and the fascinating history stretching back to ancient Greece.

History of the fortress – from Anaxilas to the Ruffo dynasty

The first fortification on Scilla's rock was erected in the 5th century BC by order of Anaxilas, tyrant of Rhegion (today's Reggio Calabria), as a defence against pirates raiding the Strait of Messina. In 390 BC the fortress was seized by Dionysius of Syracuse, and later expanded under Emperor Octavian Augustus. Pliny the Elder described this place as Oppidum Scyllaeum – a major defensive outpost controlling the crossing between Calabria and Sicily.

In 1060 the castle surrendered to Robert Guiscard, the Norman conqueror, who turned it into a military garrison. Over the following centuries the fortress changed hands repeatedly, until in 1533 Paolo Ruffo purchased the castle and made it his princely residence. The Ruffo family gave the fortress its present name and carried out a thorough renovation, adding residential chambers, a monumental gateway bearing the family coat of arms, and a courtyard with a grand staircase.

Two catastrophic earthquakes – in 1783 and 1908 – severely damaged the castle. Reconstruction took decades, but thanks to those efforts Castello Ruffo is now one of the best-preserved fortresses in Calabria. In 1913 a lighthouse was installed at the castle's summit, and it continues to guide ships entering the Strait of Messina to this day – carrying on the watchful role this rock has played for over two thousand years.

Mythology – Scylla and Charybdis from Homer's Odyssey

The rock on which Castello Ruffo stands is, according to the ancient Greeks, the lair of the sea monster Scylla – a six-headed beast that snatched sailors from passing ships. On the opposite side of the strait, off the coast of Sicily, lurked Charybdis – a whirlpool that swallowed entire vessels. In Book XII of the Odyssey, Homer describes how Odysseus, following the sorceress Circe's advice, chose the route closer to Scylla – losing six companions but saving the ship from Charybdis.

The phrase "between Scylla and Charybdis" has become a universal metaphor for choosing between two evils. Standing on the castle walls, it is easy to see where the legend came from – powerful currents, swirling waters, and a sheer cliff dropping straight into the sea create a landscape tailor-made for mythology. The castle museum contains references to this tradition, including a map of ancient shipping routes through the Strait.

What to see – architecture, museum, and panoramas

The castle has an irregular layout adapted to the terrain, yet retains the cohesive character of a fortress with curtain walls, towers, and loopholes. The entrance leads across a bridge and through a pointed-arch portal crowned with the Ruffo coat of arms. Beyond a vaulted vestibule opens a courtyard, from which a grand staircase leads up to the residential halls.

The interior now serves as a cultural centre – it houses the Regional Centre for the Recovery of Calabrian Historical Monuments, and the halls are used for temporary exhibitions and conferences. The most notable permanent exhibits include:

  • Luntre – an original fishing boat used in the traditional swordfish hunt in the Strait of Messina
  • Naturalistic exhibits – models and artefacts related to the swordfish fishing tradition
  • Lighthouse – operational since 1913, accessible as part of the castle visit

The real attraction, however, is the panorama from the defensive walls. To the north stretches the Costa Viola – a cliff-lined coast with violet hues. To the south lies the Strait of Messina with Sicily beyond, and on clear days Mount Etna's silhouette rises on the horizon. To the west, on cloudless afternoons, the outlines of the Aeolian Islands appear – including the distinctive cone of Stromboli.

Practical information

Location: Piazza Matrice, Scilla (RC) – on the rocky headland between Chianalea and Marina Grande beach
Hours (approximate): Daily 9:30–13:00 and 15:00–19:00 (may vary seasonally)
Getting there: On foot from the centre of Scilla (5 min uphill) or from Reggio Calabria (25 km, about 30 min by car)
Visit duration: Approx. 45–60 min

Frequently Asked Questions

How old is Castello Ruffo and what can you see there today?

The first fortification on this site dates to the 5th century BC. The present castle is largely the work of the Ruffo family from the 16th century, rebuilt multiple times after earthquakes. Today it houses a museum with exhibits on traditional fishing, temporary exhibitions, and a working lighthouse dating from 1913.

What is the castle's connection to Greek mythology?

The castle rock is traditionally identified as the lair of Scylla – the sea monster from Homer's Odyssey. Together with Charybdis on the Sicilian side, they formed a deadly trap for sailors passing through the Strait of Messina.

Can you see Sicily from the castle?

Yes – Sicily is just 3.2 km away and clearly visible from the walls. On clear days you can also see Mount Etna and the outlines of the Aeolian Islands, including the active volcano Stromboli.

How long does a visit to Castello Ruffo take?

A standard visit takes 45–60 minutes, including the museum exhibition and time to admire the panoramic views from the defensive walls.

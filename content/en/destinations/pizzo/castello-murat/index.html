Castello Murat is a 15th-century Aragonese fortress that entered European history as the site of Joachim Murat’s imprisonment and execution – Napoleon’s marshal, King of Naples, and one of the most tragic figures of the Napoleonic era. The castle overlooks the sea in the heart of Pizzo, and its walls house a museum dedicated to the king’s final days.

History of the castle – from coastal fortress to a stage of royal drama

The fortification dates back to 1380, when the massive cylindrical tower known as Torre Maestra was erected during the Angevin period. About a century later, Ferdinand I of Aragon expanded the structure into a full-scale fortress with a quadrangular layout and a second corner tower. The purpose was to protect the Tyrrhenian coastline against Saracen raids that regularly plagued Calabrian ports.

For centuries the castle served military and prison functions, but the events of October 1815 etched it permanently into European history. Joachim Murat, stripped of his throne after Napoleon’s fall, launched a desperate attempt to reclaim the Kingdom of Naples. With a handful of supporters he landed on the beach near Pizzo on 8 October 1815, hoping for a spontaneous popular uprising. Instead, he was captured by local militia and thrown into the castle’s dungeons.

A military tribunal convened almost immediately. The trial lasted barely an hour – the death sentence was predetermined. On 13 October 1815, Murat faced the firing squad in the castle courtyard. According to witnesses, he refused a blindfold and issued the command to fire himself, uttering his famous last words: “Aim at my chest, not my face” (Mirate al petto, non al viso). He was 48 years old.

The Murat Museum – what to see inside

The castle houses the Museo Murattiano, which recreates the king’s final days with meticulous attention to detail. The visit follows a chronological path through the stages of the drama:

Dungeons and prison cells – damp, stone chambers where Murat and his companions were held immediately after capture. The cramped interiors convey the conditions of their last days.

Second-floor chamber – this is where Murat wrote his farewell letter to his wife Caroline (Napoleon’s sister) and their four children. A preserved copy of the letter is displayed in a glass case, alongside period furniture.

Execution courtyard – where the shots were fired. A memorial plaque quotes the king’s last words. From this spot, you can see the sea and the Gulf of Sant’Eufemia – a cruel irony, as this was the horizon that never brought Murat his freedom.

Exhibition hall – uniforms, period weapons, court documents, and copies of Murat’s correspondence with Napoleon. Every four years, the “Gioacchino Murat” Cultural Association stages a historical re-enactment of the landing, capture, and execution – a spectacle that draws historians and visitors from across Europe.

Castello Murat – frequently asked questions

Who was Joachim Murat and why was he executed in Pizzo?

Joachim Murat was a Marshal of France and Napoleon Bonaparte’s brother-in-law (he married Napoleon’s sister Caroline). In 1808 Napoleon appointed him King of Naples. After the empire’s collapse, Murat attempted to reclaim his throne, landing in Pizzo on 8 October 1815. He was captured, tried by a military court, and executed by firing squad in the castle courtyard five days later.

How much does it cost to visit Castello Murat?

Adult admission is €2.50, reduced €1.50. It is one of the most affordable castle-museums in southern Italy. A visit takes approximately 45–60 minutes.

Is the castle open year-round?

Yes, Castello Murat is open throughout the year. Hours vary by season – closing at 7:00 pm in winter, and staying open until midnight in August, allowing for sunset visits overlooking the gulf.

Where is Murat’s tomb?

Murat’s tomb is in the Cathedral of San Giorgio Martire in central Pizzo, a few minutes’ walk from the castle. The Baroque church, dating from 1632, is open to visitors.

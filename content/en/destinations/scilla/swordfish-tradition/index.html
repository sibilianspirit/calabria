Swordfish fishing in the Strait of Messina is one of the oldest living fishing traditions in the Mediterranean basin – documented since the 2nd century BC and practised in virtually unchanged form to this day. Fishermen from Scilla and nearby Bagnara Calabra head out to sea on specialised boats with masts reaching 20–30 metres, spotting and harpooning swordfish as they pass through the strait during mating season. This tradition has been inscribed in the Register of Intangible Heritage of Mediterranean Countries through the iHERITAGE project.

Boats and technique – feluca, luntro, and passarella

Traditional swordfish fishing relies on the precise cooperation of two types of vessel and a small crew in which each member has a strictly defined role:

The feluca is a large mother ship fitted with a distinctive sighting mast (antenna) reaching 20–30 metres in height. At the top stands the antenniere (spotter), who scans the surface for swordfish and directs the boat's manoeuvres through shouts and hand signals. The feluca is thought to be of Phoenician origin – its design has changed little over the centuries.

The luntro is a smaller, agile boat that accompanies the feluca. When the spotter on the mast spots a swordfish, the luntro sets off in pursuit. At the bow stands the lanzatore (harpooner), who strikes the fish with a precisely aimed harpoon throw. The entire sequence – from sighting to strike – often takes just a few minutes and demands flawless coordination.

Today some boats use passarelle – steel observation platforms mounted on the bow that replace the traditional mast. It is a compromise between tradition and safety, but the classic feluca with its towering mast is still used by some fishermen.

Hunting strategy – knowledge passed down through generations

The fishermen of Scilla draw on a profound understanding of swordfish behaviour, handed down from father to son for generations. The key element of their strategy is observing mating pairs – during the breeding season (May–September) swordfish migrate through the Strait of Messina in pairs. Fishermen target the larger female first, knowing that the male will instinctively follow his mate, making a second catch easier.

The choice of fishing ground is no accident. The Strait of Messina, just 3.2 km wide at its narrowest point, forms a natural funnel through which migrating fish must pass. Strong sea currents push swordfish toward the surface, where they become visible to the spotter on the mast. It is precisely this geography that has made Scilla and neighbouring Bagnara Calabra the main centres of swordfish fishing in Europe for millennia.

The tradition also has a spiritual dimension. Masses are held for a successful season before the fishing begins, and the first swordfish caught each year is traditionally offered to the church. In Chianalea – Scilla's oldest fishing quarter – families still live who have practised this craft for five or six generations.

Swordfish in cuisine and culture – Sagra del Pesce Spada

Swordfish (pesce spada) is not just an object of the catch – it is a pillar of Calabrian cuisine and a cultural symbol of Scilla. Swordfish meat is delicate, slightly pink in colour, and suited to a wide range of culinary techniques:

  • Grilled swordfish – steaks marinated in olive oil, lemon, and oregano, grilled over charcoal
  • Involtini di pesce spada – thin rolls stuffed with breadcrumbs, capers, olives, and cheese
  • Carpaccio di pesce spada – raw, thinly sliced with olive oil and lemon
  • Pasta con pesce spada – pasta with swordfish sauce, tomatoes, capers, and olives

The climax of the season is the Sagra del Pesce Spada (Swordfish Festival), held in July in Bagnara Calabra. During the festival you can sample dozens of swordfish dishes, watch demonstrations of traditional fishing techniques, and hear the stories of veteran fishermen. It is an excellent opportunity to experience this tradition up close. An original luntro – the boat used in traditional fishing – is on display at Castello Ruffo.

Frequently Asked Questions

How old is the swordfish fishing tradition in Scilla?

The tradition is documented from the 2nd century BC, making it one of the oldest living fishing traditions in the Mediterranean basin. The harpooning technique from a tall sighting mast has remained virtually unchanged for over two thousand years.

What is a feluca and why does it have such a tall mast?

A feluca is a traditional swordfish fishing boat fitted with a sighting mast 20–30 metres high. At the top stands a spotter (antenniere) who scans for fish and directs manoeuvres. The tall mast is necessary because swordfish are only visible from the surface at a great height.

When is the Sagra del Pesce Spada held?

The Swordfish Festival takes place in July in Bagnara Calabra, a town 15 km north of Scilla. During the festival you can taste dozens of swordfish dishes and watch demonstrations of traditional fishing techniques.

Can you watch traditional swordfish fishing live?

During the season (May–September) boats with tall masts depart from Scilla's harbour in the early morning. From the waterfront or the walls of Castello Ruffo you can observe their manoeuvres at sea. Some local operators also offer tourist cruises on the Strait of Messina.

Chianalea di Scilla is one of Italy's most photogenic fishing quarters – a labyrinth of narrow lanes, stone houses built directly on the rocks, and sea that pushes between buildings like a miniature Venice. Listed among I Borghi più Belli d'Italia (The Most Beautiful Villages of Italy), Chianalea has preserved the authentic character of a fishing settlement where the rhythm of daily life is set by the catch, and restaurants serve whatever was landed that morning.

The quarter sits at the foot of Castello Ruffo, on the eastern shore of the rocky headland, sheltered from the open sea. It is the oldest part of Scilla and a place that captivates everyone who reaches it – especially at sunset, when golden light bounces off the calm waters of the Strait of Messina.

History and architecture – houses on the sea

The name Chianalea derives from canalea (or piano della galea – "galley deck"), referring directly to the quarter's most distinctive feature: sea-water channels and inlets separating the houses, where fishermen moor their boats. This characteristic earned Chianalea the nickname "Little Venice of the South" (Piccola Venezia del Sud).

The houses of Chianalea do not stand above the sea – they stand in it. The foundations of many buildings are washed by waves, and some restaurants operate on wooden platforms raised above the water on stilts, accessible both from land and by boat. This unique architecture arose from necessity – the narrow strip of rocky coastline left no room for conventional building, so fishermen built where they could: directly on cliffs and in crevices.

The most notable architectural landmarks in Chianalea include:

  • Ruffo Fountain (16th century) – bearing the coat of arms of the Ruffo family, founders of the castle
  • Fountain of Three Channels (1610) – decorated with masks and friezes, once a main source of drinking water for fishermen
  • Canalello Fountain (St. Clement's Fountain) – another historic water point
  • Church of Santa Maria di Porto Salvo – patron saint of fishermen and sailors
  • Church of San Giuseppe – a small church woven into the fishing quarter's fabric
  • Villa Zagari (1933) – an eclectic building designated a National Monument, honouring the scholar Giuseppe Zagari (1863–1946)

Life in Chianalea – fishermen, cuisine, and sunsets

Chianalea is no open-air museum – it is a living quarter where fishing families still carry on a multigenerational tradition. In the morning, boats are hauled up on tiny beaches between buildings while fishermen mend their nets. The main product of local catches is swordfish (pesce spada), hunted using traditional methods in the Strait of Messina from May to September – read more in our article on the swordfish fishing tradition.

The restaurants of Chianalea are an attraction in their own right. Several operate literally over the water – you sit on a terrace on stilts, the sea lapping beneath your feet, while grilled swordfish, carpaccio, or spaghetti alla scillese lands on your plate. According to local legend, swordfish was the only fish unafraid of the sea monster Scylla – which is why it became the culinary symbol of this town. Discover more about Calabrian cuisine in our guide.

In the evening Chianalea transforms into one of the most romantic spots on the Calabrian coast. The sunset over the Strait, with Sicily's silhouette on the horizon and warm light reflecting off stone facades, is a sight that stays with you long after you leave. The best vantage point is the waterfront at the end of the main lane, where you can see the village, the castle, and the open sea all at once.

Practical information

Location: Eastern side of the Scilla headland, at the foot of Castello Ruffo
Access: On foot from Scilla centre (Via Nazionale) – Chianalea begins past the castle
Best time to visit: Afternoon hours, especially for sunset; summer months are busier
Tip: The lanes are very narrow and steep – comfortable shoes are a must
Getting there: From Reggio Calabria about 30 min by car (25 km); train to Scilla station

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Chianalea called the "Little Venice of the South"?

The nickname comes from the sea-water channels separating the fishing houses – water pushes between buildings and boats are moored directly alongside foundations, creating a landscape reminiscent of Venetian canals, only in miniature and with open sea instead of a lagoon.

Is Chianalea listed on any official register?

Yes – Chianalea belongs to the prestigious I Borghi più Belli d'Italia network (The Most Beautiful Villages of Italy), confirming its exceptional architectural and cultural value.

What dish should you try in Chianalea?

Swordfish (pesce spada) in every form – grilled, as carpaccio, in pasta sauce, or as involtini (stuffed rolls). It is the icon of local cuisine, caught using traditional methods in the Strait of Messina.

What is the best time of day to visit Chianalea?

It is most beautiful in the afternoon and at sunset, when golden light reflects off the stone facades and calm waters. In the morning you will see the fishing life – boats, nets, and the fresh catch.
